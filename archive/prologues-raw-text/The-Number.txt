# Prologue

## The Arithmetic of Desire

There was a time when people lied.

Not merely in the ordinary ways. Not merely the lies told to employers, lovers, tax collectors, and strangers.

No.

They lied about themselves.

Or perhaps a kinder word would be *edited*.

A woman would sit across from a man on a first date and carefully remove entire chapters from her history. A husband would carry private memories to his grave. A teenager would invent experience he did not possess. A widow would conceal longing she still felt.

We called these deceptions.

But they were something far more ancient.

They were privacy.

They were the spaces between what happened and what was known.

The distance between the soul and the crowd.

Then the Number arrived.

No one remembers precisely when humanity stopped asking *why*.

For a few brief years, scientists searched desperately for answers. Religious leaders declared prophecy fulfilled. Governments convened commissions. Philosophers wrote papers. Conspiracy theorists experienced their finest hour.

And yet, after enough time passed, the mystery itself became unimportant.

The Number remained.

That was enough.

As with all enduring things, civilization ceased questioning its existence and instead reorganized itself around its presence.

A child was born.

A Number appeared.

A life unfolded.

The Number changed.

The world judged.

Simple.

Elegant.

Efficient.

Humanity has always loved efficiency.

Particularly when judging one another.

The Number glowed softly upon every forehead, visible to everyone except the person carrying it. Mirrors refused to acknowledge it. Cameras could not capture it. Only another human being could see it.

An amusing detail, if one appreciates irony.

The greatest instrument of social measurement ever devised required a witness.

A human being could not know his own Number directly.

He required another pair of eyes.

Another consciousness.

Another judge.

One suspects that if God truly invented the Number, He possessed a wicked sense of humor.

The first generation believed the Number would make society more honest.

The second believed it would make society more moral.

By the third generation, no one remembered the distinction.

What followed was entirely predictable.

Men with high Numbers became legends.

Women with high Numbers became warnings.

Men with low Numbers became punchlines.

Women with low Numbers became prizes.

Entire industries emerged around interpreting what the Number supposedly revealed about character.

Books were written.

Universities created departments.

Governments drafted policies.

Corporations designed algorithms.

Religions found new scripture hidden inside old scripture.

The Number was discussed endlessly.

Its meaning was debated constantly.

Its authority was questioned rarely.

That is the peculiar thing about measurements.

People may argue over what they mean.

Very few stop to ask whether they should exist at all.

The Number transformed intimacy into information.

And information, once created, develops an appetite.

Soon the Number was not enough.

People wanted categories.

Predictions.

Compatibility scores.

Behavioral forecasts.

Statistical models of love.

They wanted certainty.

Human beings have always mistaken certainty for wisdom.

A dangerous confusion.

History is littered with the corpses of those two ideas.

The tragedy, however, was not the Number.

The tragedy was what humanity discovered about itself once the Number appeared.

We learned that transparency does not eliminate prejudice.

It perfects it.

We learned that data does not abolish stereotypes.

It provides new machinery for them.

We learned that even when everyone possesses identical access to information, they will still find ways to divide the worthy from the unworthy.

The pure from the impure.

The chosen from the condemned.

The Number promised truth.

Instead, it revealed interpretation.

A distinction civilization spent decades failing to understand.

For the Number never actually told a story.

It merely counted events.

Human beings supplied the narrative afterward.

A Number of three became innocence.

A Number of three hundred became corruption.

A Number of zero became either holiness or failure, depending upon who happened to be looking.

The Number itself remained silent.

The crowd did the talking.

As crowds always do.

Loudly.

Confidently.

Incorrectly.

And so the years passed.

Children grew into adults.

Adults became symbols.

Symbols became statistics.

Statistics became policy.

An entire civilization slowly forgot the difference between a person and a measurement.

Until one day, a professor with a Number of zero met a woman whose Number read three hundred sixty-seven.

By every accepted metric, they represented opposite ends of a social spectrum.

One was considered untouched.

The other overdefined.

One invisible.

The other impossible to ignore.

Neither fit the story assigned to them.

Which is often how revolutions begin.

Not with violence.

Not with ideology.

Not with grand declarations.

But with an encounter.

A contradiction.

A single moment when reality refuses to obey the narrative constructed around it.

The Number counted many things.

But it could not count that.

No system ever can.

For there remains one aspect of humanity that has always resisted measurement.

The thing every civilization attempts to quantify.

The thing every civilization ultimately fails to contain.

The thing hiding behind every statistic, every law, every judgment, every glowing digit.

The human story itself.

And stories, unlike Numbers, rarely stay where they are told.
