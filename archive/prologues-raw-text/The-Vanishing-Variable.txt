Prologue
On the Things We Mistake for Permanent
The Arrogance of Familiarity
There is a peculiar arrogance that accompanies familiarity.
It is not loud. It does not march or announce itself. It simply settles in. It becomes part of the furniture, occupying the corners of a room so completely that, after a time, no one remembers it ever arrived.
We assume that what has always been there will remain. We count on the streetlight outside the window, the tree at the end of the block, the old woman who opens her shop at precisely the same hour each morning, the river, the church, the city itself.
We rarely notice these things while they are present; we notice only their interruption.
•	A light bulb burns out.
•	A tree falls.
•	A store closes.
Then, for a brief moment, absence acquires shape. We point to the empty space and say, “Something is missing.”
But even then, we misunderstand what has happened. The thing itself is rarely what troubles us. What troubles us is the realization that we had quietly built part of ourselves around its existence.
The tree was shade. The shop was routine. The river was orientation.
What we thought was external had, in fact, become structural. Its absence reveals a dependency we never consented to examine.
How Civilizations Explain Loss
Civilizations suffer from this blindness more than individuals. A person can mourn honestly; a civilization prefers explanation.
When confronted with loss, it asks questions not to understand but to restore comfort:
•	What happened?
•	Who is responsible?
•	How do we fix it?
The answers matter less than the reassurance that an answer exists. Uncertainty is intolerable to large systems.
And so societies become storytellers. They invent causes, assign blame, and construct myths sturdy enough to bear the weight of their contradictions.
Those stories become useful. Then necessary. Then sacred. Eventually, they cease to be stories at all. They become common sense, inherited truth, the air itself.
Generations are born into them. Children learn them before they learn history. Adults defend them without remembering where they came from. Institutions organize themselves around them, and entire professions emerge to refine them.
A civilization can spend centuries living inside a story so completely that it forgets the difference between explanation and reality. This works remarkably well—until reality changes. Or, more accurately, until reality refuses to continue cooperating.
The Misunderstanding of Power
History teaches us that empires often misunderstand the source of their strength. They mistake extraction for production, control for stability, and dependence for superiority.
The machine runs, and they conclude they built it. The harvest arrives, and they conclude they grew it. The music plays, and they conclude they composed it.
Then one day, something subtle occurs. Not a revolution. Not a war. Not a collapse. Something quieter.
A pressure releases. A thread loosens. A relationship long mistaken for permanence begins to withdraw.
At first, no one notices. The machine still runs. The harvest still arrives. The music still plays. Life continues.
That is the genius of gradual change: it disguises itself as continuity. The familiar remains familiar enough that the mind refuses alarm.
A missing face becomes coincidence. An empty seat becomes scheduling. A vanished family becomes relocation. The explanation arrives before the observation, and because the explanation is comforting, it is accepted.
Years pass. The pattern deepens. The absences accumulate. Still, the system persists.
This persistence becomes evidence. If nothing has collapsed, then surely nothing important has changed. If the city still functions, then surely the losses are insignificant. If tomorrow resembles yesterday, then surely yesterday remains intact.
The Invisible Economy
But beneath every functioning society lies an invisible economy—not merely of money, but of meaning, blame, responsibility, and sacrifice.
Every civilization decides, consciously or not, where it will place its burdens. Every civilization develops mechanisms to protect itself from self-examination.
•	Some build monuments.
•	Some build myths.
•	Some build enemies.
The arrangement is rarely acknowledged. Its participants often do not understand it. That is why it can endure for generations.
The most powerful contracts are not written; they are inherited. And the most dangerous contracts are those in which one party performs a function so completely that the other forgets the function is being performed at all.
Until the function ceases.
Then confusion arrives—not because something new has happened, but because something old has stopped.
The Question at the Center
This is the story of such a stopping.
Not a catastrophe. Not an extinction. Not even a disappearance in the ordinary sense.
It is the story of a society confronting a possibility it never prepared itself to consider:
•	What if the thing you blamed for your problems was never the cause?
•	What if the thing you depended upon most was the thing you understood least?
•	What if an absence could reveal more than a presence ever could?
•	What becomes of a civilization when the explanation it inherited begins, quietly and without permission, to leave?
The answers are not contained in these pages. Only the consequences are. The answers, as always, belong to the reader.
