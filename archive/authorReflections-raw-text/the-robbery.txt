# Author's Reflection

## On *The Robbery*

There is a particular kind of loneliness that does not arrive through absence.

It arrives through protection.

This novella began with a question that unsettled me the more I considered it:

**What if the greatest thief in our lives is not the person who leaves, but the part of ourselves that emerges afterward?**

Most stories about heartbreak concern loss. Someone leaves. Someone dies. Someone betrays. The wound is obvious because the source of the wound is visible.

But I have become increasingly interested in a quieter phenomenon.

The transformation.

The subtle ways people reorganize themselves around pain.

A failed relationship does not merely end. It often creates an institution. It establishes policies. It drafts constitutions. It erects borders. It appoints guards.

One day a person experiences loss.

Years later, they discover they have become its custodian.

That observation became the seed of *The Robbery*.

The protagonist genuinely believes he has been robbed.

And perhaps he has.

Love is not without casualties.

Relationships do take things from us. They consume time, innocence, optimism, certainty, trust, and sometimes entire futures we once imagined. Most readers will recognize this immediately because most readers have their own inventory of losses.

The challenge was not determining whether something had been taken.

The challenge was determining what happened next.

What interested me was the possibility that the original wound eventually becomes secondary to the defenses built around it.

A castle erected to survive a siege may remain standing long after the war has ended.

The soldiers leave.

The enemy disappears.

The threat fades into memory.

Yet the walls remain.

Eventually, the inhabitants forget why the walls were built in the first place.

At that point, the fortress no longer protects life.

It organizes it.

I began wondering whether many of the things we call maturity are simply well-decorated forms of fear.

How much wisdom is actually caution?

How much caution is actually grief?

How much grief has quietly become identity?

The central metaphor of the novella—a man standing trial for "defending empty space"—emerged from this line of questioning.

I was fascinated by the idea that a person could spend decades protecting something that no longer exists.

Not because they are irrational.

But because they are rational.

The protagonist's arguments are compelling.

They are logical.

They are evidence-based.

He can point to names, dates, disappointments, betrayals, and departures.

His case is persuasive because pain often is.

Pain rarely struggles to justify itself.

Yet the court's accusation remains:

What if your defenses have outlived the danger?

What if the locks remain long after the thieves have gone?

What if the prison and the sanctuary have become indistinguishable?

These questions eventually led me to the character I did not initially intend to write:

Emptiness.

Of all the figures in the novella, Emptiness became the most interesting to me because it refuses to behave like a villain.

It does not attack.

It does not deceive.

It does not force itself upon the protagonist.

Instead, it helps him survive.

It stays when others leave.

It offers stability when life feels chaotic.

It asks very little in return.

In many ways, Emptiness is the ideal companion.

And that is precisely what makes it dangerous.

We often imagine loneliness as something we endure.

But what if loneliness can also become something we trust?

What if, after enough disappointments, absence begins to feel safer than possibility?

What if solitude becomes less a condition and more a relationship?

Those questions pushed the story beyond heartbreak and into something closer to existential inquiry.

The novella gradually became less interested in romance and more interested in freedom.

Because beneath every lock lies a trade.

We surrender uncertainty.

In exchange, we gain control.

We surrender vulnerability.

In exchange, we gain predictability.

We surrender possibility.

In exchange, we gain safety.

The transaction feels reasonable.

Until one day we discover what the exchange truly cost.

This realization ultimately transformed my understanding of the title.

The story begins with a man convinced he was robbed by others.

It ends with a man recognizing that the deeper theft occurred within himself.

Fear became the thief.

Control became the getaway vehicle.

And freedom became the missing property.

This is why I chose to include *The Robbery* in *The Commodified Self*.

Most of the novellas in this collection explore how society commodifies human beings.

How markets commodify desire.

How institutions commodify identity.

How culture commodifies race, beauty, intelligence, intimacy, or memory.

*The Robbery* examines something more intimate.

The commodification of the soul.

The protagonist transforms trust into a scarce resource.

He treats vulnerability as a liability.

He manages emotional exposure as though it were financial risk.

He becomes both owner and auditor of his own heart.

In doing so, he turns himself into a marketplace.

The tragedy is not that he loses love.

The tragedy is that he begins accounting for life instead of living it.

Yet I did not want the novella to end in despair.

Because I do not believe emptiness is merely an enemy.

Sometimes emptiness keeps us alive.

Sometimes withdrawal is necessary.

Sometimes the walls are justified.

The danger occurs when temporary shelter becomes permanent residence.

The danger occurs when survival becomes identity.

The danger occurs when we forget that the purpose of protection is eventually to stop protecting.

To lower the drawbridge.

To unlock the gate.

To risk the robbery again.

The older I become, the more I suspect that courage is not the absence of fear.

Nor is it the certainty that things will end well.

Courage may simply be the willingness to remain vulnerable after acquiring every reason not to.

That is the question at the heart of this novella.

Not whether we have been robbed.

Most of us have.

The question is whether we intend to spend the rest of our lives guarding the scene of the crime.

— **Alphonse Damas**
