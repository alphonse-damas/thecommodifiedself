Author's Reflection
On Borrowed Desire and Borrowed Worth

When I began writing Proxy, I thought I was writing a story about loneliness.

I finished it realizing it was a story about legitimacy.

Loneliness is the surface wound.

Legitimacy is the deeper one.

The world of Proxy emerges from a question that felt increasingly relevant as I observed modern life:

What happens when people stop wanting intimacy and start wanting proof?

Proof that they matter.

Proof that they are desirable.

Proof that they are visible.

Proof that they belong.

The technology in this novella allows millions of people to experience what it feels like to be desired through another person's nervous system. At first glance, this appears to be a story about romance, attraction, or sexuality.

It is not.

The subscribers are not chasing sex.

They are chasing relief.

Relief from the suspicion that something is fundamentally wrong with them.

Relief from self-consciousness.

Relief from the exhausting burden of measuring themselves against the world's response.

That distinction became increasingly important as the story unfolded.

Elias believes he wants what Lucien has.

He believes he wants the women.

The attention.

The effortless conversations.

The warmth.

But what he truly wants is what he imagines those things mean.

Validation.

Authorization.

A license to exist without apology.

The tragedy is that Proxy appears to provide exactly that.

For a few hours, subscribers can inhabit a man whose existence is welcomed before it is evaluated.

A man who enters rooms without bracing for rejection.

A man who moves through life without constantly interpreting himself.

The experience feels liberating because it suspends self-surveillance.

For a brief moment, people stop asking:

Am I enough?

And perhaps that is the most addictive feeling in the world.

Throughout the novella, Marcus serves as a philosophical counterweight to this desire.

Marcus is often misunderstood.

He is not a man who has transcended loneliness.

He is not a man who no longer desires affection.

He is not a man immune to rejection.

He is simply a man who refuses to treat rejection as revelation.

That distinction matters.

Modern society increasingly encourages us to experience ourselves through external systems.

Followers.

Likes.

Status.

Income.

Attention.

Visibility.

Desirability.

We are taught to view ourselves through mirrors constructed by other people.

Eventually we forget that the mirror and the self are not the same thing.

Proxy merely pushes this tendency to its logical conclusion.

Instead of measuring ourselves through another person's reaction, we simply rent another person's reality altogether.

The technology is futuristic.

The impulse is ancient.

One of the central concerns of The Commodified Self anthology is what happens when aspects of human identity become economic products.

In some stories, labor is commodified.

In others, culture.

In others, history.

In Proxy, what becomes commodified is emotional legitimacy itself.

The product is not desire.

The product is relief from feeling undesirable.

The product is not intimacy.

The product is relief from feeling invisible.

The product is not connection.

The product is relief from self-rejection.

This is why I resisted portraying Proxy as purely evil.

The platform genuinely helps people.

It lowers suffering.

It eases loneliness.

It provides comfort.

Many users benefit from it.

That ambiguity is important.

Most transformative technologies are not adopted because they make life worse.

They are adopted because they solve a real problem.

The danger emerges when a solution quietly transforms our understanding of ourselves.

By the end of the novella, Elias discovers something uncomfortable.

His greatest dependency was never Proxy.

His greatest dependency was the belief that another person's desire could determine his value.

Proxy simply gave that belief a delivery mechanism.

That realization extends beyond the fictional world of this story.

How many people today organize their lives around being seen?

How many measure themselves through attention?

How many experience every social interaction as evidence for or against their worth?

How many secretly believe that love, attraction, success, or popularity can answer questions that are ultimately philosophical rather than social?

These questions interested me far more than the technology itself.

Because technology changes.

Human hunger rarely does.

If Proxy has a central thesis, perhaps it is this:

The world's response to you and your relationship to yourself are not the same thing.

Confusing those two things creates suffering.

Building economies around that confusion creates industries.

Building identities around that confusion creates dependence.

And building civilizations around that confusion may create entire generations who no longer know how to belong to themselves without permission from someone else.

Perhaps that is the real danger explored in this novella.

Not that people borrowed another man's memories.

Not that they borrowed another man's desires.

But that they slowly forgot they were allowed to be human before receiving anyone else's approval.

— Alphonse Damas
