# Author's Reflection

## On Invisible People

One of the questions that inspired this novella arrived disguised as a joke.

Who cleans Galt's toilets?

It is the sort of question that sounds clever at first and profound only later.

Most people immediately understand what the question is attacking. It challenges the fantasy that a society can be sustained entirely by exceptional individuals. It asks us to consider the workers who rarely appear in grand narratives about innovation, progress, genius, or wealth.

But as I spent more time with the idea, I became increasingly dissatisfied with that interpretation.

Because the question itself contains an assumption.

It assumes that the people cleaning the toilets are somehow less important than the people designing the future.

The longer I examined that assumption, the less convincing it became.

Civilization possesses a peculiar habit.

It rewards visibility.

The more visible a contribution becomes, the more valuable we tend to believe it is.

Inventors become legends.

Entrepreneurs become icons.

Artists become cultural symbols.

Political leaders become historical figures.

Entire industries emerge to celebrate extraordinary people doing extraordinary things.

Yet every achievement rests upon a mountain of ordinary labor so vast that it becomes nearly impossible to see.

No skyscraper stands because of the architect alone.

No hospital functions because of the surgeon alone.

No company survives because of the founder alone.

No civilization persists because of its visionaries alone.

Someone cleans.

Someone repairs.

Someone delivers.

Someone maintains.

Someone performs the thousands of repetitive tasks that allow everyone else to imagine themselves independent.

The irony is difficult to ignore.

The more successful a society becomes, the easier it becomes to overlook the people who keep it functioning.

Success creates distance from maintenance.

And distance creates illusion.

Eventually people begin believing that systems sustain themselves.

They do not.

Nothing sustains itself.

Not institutions.

Not economies.

Not families.

Not relationships.

Not civilizations.

Everything survives because someone, somewhere, continues performing work that rarely receives applause.

This realization became the emotional center of *Who Cleans Galt's Toilets?*

The novella is not an attack on excellence.

Far from it.

Human progress depends upon excellence.

Innovation matters.

Creativity matters.

Ambition matters.

Achievement matters.

The world is undeniably improved by people who push beyond existing limits.

The problem arises when achievement becomes the only contribution we recognize.

A culture that celebrates only creation eventually forgets the importance of maintenance.

And maintenance is where most of human life occurs.

We spend remarkably little time building things.

We spend most of our lives sustaining them.

A marriage is easier to begin than maintain.

A business is easier to launch than sustain.

A community is easier to establish than preserve.

Even freedom itself often requires less effort to win than to maintain.

History repeatedly demonstrates this truth.

The dramatic moment receives the monument.

The daily sacrifice receives the routine.

One becomes legend.

The other becomes expectation.

And because it becomes expected, it gradually disappears from view.

I suspect this pattern extends beyond economics.

It shapes the way we view one another.

Modern culture increasingly encourages people to ask a particular question:

What makes you exceptional?

It is not an unreasonable question.

But it is incomplete.

An equally important question rarely receives attention:

What makes you dependable?

Dependability lacks glamour.

Nobody writes motivational speeches about showing up consistently for thirty years.

No statues are erected to reliability.

No documentaries celebrate the person who quietly prevented disaster by doing their job every day.

And yet most functioning societies depend more heavily upon dependable people than extraordinary ones.

This is not because greatness is unimportant.

It is because greatness is rare.

Dependability is scalable.

Civilization cannot be built entirely from exceptional individuals.

There are never enough of them.

What civilization can be built from is ordinary people who accept responsibility for tasks that others would rather ignore.

That realization eventually transformed the novella.

The story began as an investigation into value.

It became an investigation into dignity.

Specifically, whether dignity should be attached to status or contribution.

Those are not always the same thing.

A society may reward one while depending upon the other.

A civilization may celebrate one while quietly collapsing without the other.

The tension between those realities fascinates me because it mirrors a broader human tendency.

We often confuse importance with recognition.

The two are not synonymous.

Some of the most important people in our lives receive the least recognition.

Parents.

Caregivers.

Custodians.

Teachers.

Maintenance workers.

Nurses.

The countless individuals whose labor becomes visible only when it disappears.

Perhaps that is why the question continues to linger.

Who cleans Galt's toilets?

Not because the answer diminishes greatness.

Because the answer reveals something greatness often forgets.

Every achievement depends upon a foundation.

Every foundation depends upon maintenance.

And maintenance depends upon people.

Real people.

Invisible people.

People whose names rarely appear in history books.

People whose absence would be noticed long before their presence is appreciated.

If this novella succeeds, I hope it encourages readers to reconsider what they mean by value.

Not market value.

Not economic value.

Human value.

The value of work that preserves rather than transforms.

The value of labor that sustains rather than innovates.

The value of people whose contributions are measured not by how high they rise but by how much of the world remains standing because they showed up.

Civilization has never struggled to admire its heroes.

The greater challenge is learning to see its caretakers.

The people who clean the toilets.

And everything else we depend upon.
