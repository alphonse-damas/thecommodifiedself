Author's Reflection
The Mirror Doesn't Work: The Gaze We Inherited

Of all the novellas in The Commodified Self, this may be the one that changed the most while I was writing it.

It did not begin as a story about internalized oppression.

It began as a story about surveillance.

Like many people, I am fascinated by the growing ability of institutions, technologies, and algorithms to observe, categorize, predict, and shape human behavior. I wondered what might happen if those systems became so sophisticated that they no longer merely watched us, but actively participated in constructing our identities.

The original premise was straightforward: a society governed by mirrors that measured worth.

The villains were obvious.

The system was oppressive.

The heroes were those who resisted it.

The ending, at first, looked like liberation.

Then something began to bother me.

The more I thought about the story, the less interested I became in the mirrors themselves.

Because history is filled with people who successfully destroyed systems only to reproduce them in new forms.

Empires collapse.

Hierarchies return.

Revolutions succeed.

New elites emerge.

The uniforms change.

The logic remains.

Eventually I realized I was asking the wrong question.

The question was never:

"What happens when the mirrors stop working?"

The more troubling question was:

"What happens if the mirrors stop working, but the people still need them?"

That realization changed everything.

The true subject of this novella became the difference between oppression and internalization.

External oppression is relatively easy to identify. It has institutions, laws, technologies, gatekeepers, and visible structures.

Internalized oppression is far more difficult to confront because it disguises itself as common sense.

It survives inside habits.

Inside aspirations.

Inside definitions of success.

Inside ideas about beauty, intelligence, desirability, respectability, and worth.

Most importantly, it survives inside the gaze.

The gaze is not merely how others see us.

It is how we learn to see ourselves through the eyes of others.

It is inherited.

Passed from generation to generation.

Sometimes consciously.

More often unconsciously.

Long after the original conditions that created it have disappeared.

The mirrors in this story are therefore symbols.

But they are not symbols of technology.

They are symbols of validation.

The need to be seen.

The need to be approved.

The need to have someone else confirm that we matter.

At first glance, those desires appear harmless.

Human even.

Perhaps they are.

But I became interested in what happens when those desires become foundational.

What happens when a person's identity depends more upon reflection than self-knowledge?

What happens when communities organize themselves around recognition rather than understanding?

What happens when entire generations inherit a way of seeing themselves that originated in systems designed to diminish them?

These questions are particularly relevant to discussions of race, identity, and historical trauma.

Many forms of oppression do not survive because the original oppressors remain in power.

They survive because the underlying assumptions become normalized.

The external structure becomes an internal reflex.

The cage becomes a habit.

The gaze becomes an inheritance.

One image guided me throughout the writing process.

A bird born in a cage.

The cage eventually breaks.

The door opens.

The bird flies free.

Yet sometime later, it begins gathering twigs and wire.

Not to build a nest.

To rebuild the cage.

Not because it loves captivity.

Because it misses the certainty.

The familiarity.

The feeling of knowing where it belongs.

That image became the emotional center of this novella.

Not because it is about weakness.

Not because it is about failure.

But because it is about a profoundly human temptation.

Freedom is often celebrated.

Much less attention is paid to the responsibilities freedom imposes.

Without systems to define us, we must define ourselves.

Without mirrors to tell us who we are, we must learn to know ourselves directly.

That is far more difficult than rebellion.

The characters in this story eventually discover that destroying the mirror is easier than overcoming the desire for reflection.

That realization transforms what appears to be a liberation story into a tragedy.

Not because the characters fail.

But because they succeed.

They destroy the system.

Then discover the system has left copies of itself behind.

Inside memory.

Inside ritual.

Inside language.

Inside themselves.

If there is a lesson in this novella, it is not that oppression is imaginary.

Nor is it that systems do not matter.

Systems matter enormously.

But dismantling systems and dismantling the assumptions they leave behind are not the same task.

One is political.

The other is psychological.

One can happen in a generation.

The other may require many.

Ultimately, The Mirror Doesn't Work asks a question I do not pretend to answer:

If we remove every external mechanism that tells us who we are, what remains?

The answer, I suspect, determines whether we become free.

Or whether we simply build new mirrors and call them something else.

— Alphonse Damas
