Author's Reflection
On In the Public Interest

Every story begins with a question.

Sometimes the writer knows the question from the beginning.

Sometimes the story reveals it along the way.

In the Public Interest began with a simple thought experiment:

What happens when a population possesses something the rest of the world desperately needs?

At first, the answer seemed obvious.

Negotiation.

Compensation.

Partnership.

Cooperation.

But stories have a habit of following questions farther than their authors initially intend.

The deeper I traveled into this one, the less interested I became in the resource itself.

The resource was never the point.

The point was what happened after people discovered they possessed it.

More specifically, what happened after they discovered they could withhold access to it.

The distinction matters.

Throughout history, many groups have contributed value to the societies around them.

Far fewer have possessed leverage.

The two are not the same.

Value can be appreciated.

Leverage must be negotiated.

And negotiation inevitably raises uncomfortable questions about power.

The moment a person can say no—and have that no matter—the relationship changes.

That realization sits at the heart of this novella.

The aliens, the vaccine, the extraction program, and the global emergency all exist to illuminate a single question:

What happens when consent becomes inconvenient?

Most societies celebrate consent when consent aligns with existing institutions.

The more difficult test occurs when consent collides with necessity.

When the stakes become enormous.

When lives depend upon cooperation.

When refusing carries consequences not only for the individual, but for everyone else.

It is easy to defend principles in ordinary times.

The challenge emerges when extraordinary circumstances arrive.

That is where In the Public Interest lives.

The title itself was chosen deliberately.

Few phrases sound more reasonable than "in the public interest."

Few phrases sound more responsible.

Few phrases appear more often when governments, corporations, and institutions justify extraordinary actions.

The phrase is not inherently sinister.

Many things genuinely are done in the public interest.

The problem arises when no one asks who defines the public interest.

Or who bears the cost of pursuing it.

Those questions become especially important when the burden and the benefit are distributed unequally.

As the story developed, another theme emerged.

Not race.

Not power.

Authority.

The distinction is important.

Power can compel behavior.

Authority seeks legitimacy.

The conflict between the government and the Compact is ultimately a conflict between two different understandings of authority.

One derives authority from necessity.

The other derives authority from consent.

Neither position is entirely absurd.

Neither position is entirely comfortable.

That tension interested me far more than any particular policy debate.

I wanted to explore what happens when both sides believe they are acting responsibly.

When both sides believe they are protecting something valuable.

When both sides can make reasonable arguments.

The result is not a struggle between good and evil.

It is a struggle between competing visions of legitimacy.

The generational conflict within Black America became one of my favorite aspects of the story.

Too often, fiction treats communities as though they speak with a single voice.

Real communities rarely do.

Different generations inherit different relationships with power.

Different experiences produce different priorities.

One generation may focus on inclusion.

Another may focus on ownership.

One may see participation as victory.

Another may ask who controls the institution they are being invited into.

Neither perspective invalidates the other.

Both emerge from lived experience.

The conversation between them felt essential to the story.

Readers may interpret this novella primarily as a story about race.

That interpretation is understandable.

Race provides the lens through which the narrative operates.

Yet I hope readers also recognize that the questions extend beyond race.

The same tensions appear whenever institutions depend upon people whose interests are not fully represented within those institutions.

The same questions emerge around labor, data, land, citizenship, knowledge, and governance.

Different contexts.

The same underlying structure.

Who decides?

Ultimately, I do not believe In the Public Interest is a story about a vaccine.

Nor do I believe it is primarily a story about aliens.

The vaccine is a flashlight.

The aliens are witnesses.

The real subject is the relationship between consent and authority.

Every society eventually discovers what it truly believes about that relationship.

Not during ordinary times.

During emergencies.

During crises.

During moments when principles become inconvenient.

Those moments reveal more than any speech ever can.

The visitors arrive seeking a cure.

They find one.

What they discover afterward may be more important.

Because diseases eventually fade.

Resources eventually change.

Emergencies eventually end.

The question remains.

Who decides?

And perhaps more importantly:

What happens when someone says no?

— Alphonse Damas
