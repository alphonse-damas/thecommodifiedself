Author's Reflection
On Visibility, Validation, and the Price of Being Seen

When I began writing Blackmail, Black Male, I thought I was writing about race.

By the time I finished, I realized I was writing about dependency.

Not economic dependency.

Not political dependency.

Psychological dependency.

The dependency that forms when a people become accustomed to measuring themselves through the eyes of those who neither know them nor love them.

The novella's title is intentionally unsettling.

At first glance, readers often assume it refers to coercion.

Manipulation.

Threats.

The forcing of one person to do something against his will.

But the deeper meaning emerged as I continued writing.

What if the blackmail isn't something being done to the black male?

What if the blackmail is the condition itself?

What if the demand is simple:

Become what we need you to be, or disappear.

The tragedy is that most people never hear the demand spoken aloud.

They absorb it through repetition.

Through incentives.

Through rewards.

Through punishment.

Through applause.

Through silence.

Over time, the demand becomes indistinguishable from identity.

The person no longer asks who he is.

He asks who he is expected to be.

That distinction sits at the heart of this story.

One of the ideas I wanted to explore was the difference between oppression and internalized oppression.

Oppression is easy to identify.

It has villains.

Laws.

Policies.

Institutions.

Historical events.

Internalized oppression is far more elusive.

Its most dangerous feature is that it no longer requires an oppressor.

The prison begins operating itself.

The surveillance becomes self-surveillance.

The policing becomes self-policing.

The individual voluntarily maintains the system because he mistakes it for reality.

This phenomenon is not unique to Black Americans.

It appears throughout history.

Among colonized populations.

Religious minorities.

Immigrant communities.

The poor.

The wealthy.

Even nations themselves.

Groups often continue enforcing outdated narratives long after the original conditions have changed.

The chains survive because they become cultural habits.

That observation became the foundation of this novella.

Throughout the story, the central question is not whether the protagonist is free.

It is whether he knows what freedom would even look like.

That may sound like a philosophical question.

It is not.

It is profoundly practical.

Imagine spending your entire life responding to expectations.

Family expectations.

Community expectations.

Political expectations.

Media expectations.

Historical expectations.

At some point, a frightening possibility emerges:

What if the person beneath all those expectations has never been introduced?

What if the self remains undiscovered because performance has become permanent?

That possibility fascinated me.

Because modern society increasingly rewards performance.

Not merely among Black Americans.

Among everyone.

Social media rewards performance.

Politics rewards performance.

Professional life rewards performance.

Entire industries exist to teach people how to become more marketable versions of themselves.

The result is a world where authenticity often loses to visibility.

And visibility becomes confused with value.

The black male occupies a unique position within this dynamic.

He is simultaneously hyper-visible and poorly understood.

Observed constantly.

Interpreted constantly.

Discussed constantly.

Yet rarely encountered as an individual.

Instead, he often appears as a symbol.

A warning.

A victim.

A threat.

A hero.

A statistic.

A demographic.

A narrative device.

Anything except a person.

The novella asks what happens when a person spends generations being described rather than known.

Eventually, the description becomes inheritance.

Children inherit stories about themselves before they inherit understanding of themselves.

Some embrace those stories.

Some resist them.

Most oscillate between the two.

That tension drives much of the emotional gravity of this work.

Another theme running beneath the surface is the relationship between recognition and power.

Many social movements begin with a legitimate desire to be seen.

To be acknowledged.

To be recognized as fully human.

History shows the necessity of that struggle.

Yet recognition contains a hidden danger.

Once a community becomes dependent upon recognition, those who control recognition acquire extraordinary influence.

The power to define reality.

The power to determine legitimacy.

The power to decide who is visible and who remains invisible.

In this way, the search for liberation can quietly evolve into a search for approval.

And approval is a fragile foundation upon which to build identity.

Approval can be granted.

Approval can be withheld.

Approval can be conditioned.

Approval can be weaponized.

Freedom requires something more durable.

It requires self-definition.

The novella's most uncomfortable suggestion is that a people may win many external battles while losing an internal one.

They may gain representation while losing autonomy.

Gain visibility while losing individuality.

Gain access while losing direction.

The contradiction is intentional.

History is filled with examples of groups achieving victories they later struggled to interpret.

Success often reveals problems failure conceals.

When barriers disappear, individuals must confront questions previously answered for them.

Who are we now?

What remains?

What do we build next?

The story offers no definitive answers.

That omission is deliberate.

Because I suspect the answer differs for every reader.

Some readers will see a critique of racism.

Others will see a critique of victimhood.

Others will see a critique of modern identity itself.

All of these interpretations are valid.

The novella was never intended to prescribe.

It was intended to provoke.

To create discomfort.

To force reflection.

To invite questions many people instinctively avoid.

The title itself contains a dual meaning.

The first meaning is obvious.

The second is more important.

Blackmail implies leverage.

A pressure point.

Something valuable being held hostage.

The question the novella asks is simple:

What exactly is being held hostage?

Reputation?

Identity?

Belonging?

History?

Dignity?

Or something deeper?

My own answer changed repeatedly while writing.

By the end, I had arrived at a different conclusion.

The thing most often held hostage is not opportunity.

It is self-perception.

Control how a person sees himself and you rarely need to control anything else.

That lesson applies to individuals.

Communities.

Institutions.

Even nations.

Perhaps especially nations.

Ultimately, Blackmail, Black Male is not a story about Black men.

Not exclusively.

It is a story about every human being who has inherited a script and mistaken it for a self.

It is about the cost of performing an identity long after its usefulness has expired.

It is about the danger of confusing attention with understanding.

And it is about the difficult, often lonely task of becoming something other than what history expected.

If there is hope within these pages, it rests in a simple possibility:

That the self is larger than the stories told about it.

And that freedom begins the moment a person becomes curious enough to find out.
