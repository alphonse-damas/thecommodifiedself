# Author's Reflection

Of all the novellas in *The Commodified Self*, this one may be the most dangerous.

Not because of what it says.

Because of what it refuses to say.

When I began writing this story, I was not interested in race as a political subject. Political arguments are abundant. Statistics are abundant. Explanations are abundant. Entire industries have emerged around producing increasingly sophisticated explanations for why society succeeds, why it fails, and who should bear responsibility for either outcome.

I became interested in something much simpler.

I became interested in the role explanations themselves play in preserving a society.

Every civilization develops stories about itself. Some are noble. Some are tragic. Most are useful. Over time, these stories become so deeply embedded in the culture that they cease to feel like stories at all. They become assumptions. Inherited truths. The lens through which reality is interpreted.

What fascinated me was the possibility that some of society's most durable explanations might not exist because they are accurate, but because they are useful.

Useful to whom is a separate question.

This novella emerged from a thought experiment:

What would happen if one of a society's oldest explanations quietly disappeared?

Not dramatically. Not through violence. Not through revolution. Not through victory or defeat.

What if it simply withdrew?

And what if, after that withdrawal, everything society claimed would improve remained stubbornly unchanged?

The story's central question is often misunderstood as being about Black disappearance.

It is not.

The disappearance is merely the mechanism.

The actual subject is dependency.

Specifically, the ways societies become dependent upon people they simultaneously marginalize, fear, exploit, romanticize, blame, and misunderstand.

The relationship explored in this novella is intentionally uncomfortable because it resembles something familiar: an abusive relationship that persists long after both parties recognize its destructive nature.

Most abusive relationships survive because they serve a function.

One person receives power.

The other receives predictability.

One receives control.

The other receives belonging.

The arrangement may be harmful, but it is stable.

Until one party withdraws.

Then everyone discovers what the relationship was actually doing.

That realization sits at the center of this book.

Throughout the writing process, I found myself returning to a question that grew increasingly difficult to answer:

What if a society's greatest dependency is something it insists it does not need?

History offers many examples of groups being blamed for a nation's problems. Far fewer examples explore what happens when those groups are removed from the equation and the problems remain.

This novella attempts to inhabit that contradiction.

Readers sometimes ask whether the disappearance represents extinction, liberation, escape, or punishment.

My answer is always the same:

I do not know.

More importantly, I do not believe the story benefits from knowing.

One of the reasons the disappearance remains open-ended is because definitive answers often become a form of comfort. They allow readers to file away uncertainty and return to ordinary life unchanged.

I was less interested in providing comfort than in creating a sustained encounter with uncertainty.

The moment we learn exactly where the disappeared have gone, the conversation shifts away from the society they left behind.

That shift felt dishonest.

This story is not ultimately about those who disappeared.

It is about those who remained.

The novella asks readers to consider a possibility that many societies throughout history have resisted:

What if the people we blame for our problems are performing functions we have never bothered to understand?

What if our explanations are protecting us from self-examination?

What if accountability becomes possible only after our excuses are gone?

These questions are not directed at any one race, ideology, institution, or nation. They are directed at human beings generally.

Projection is not a racial phenomenon.

It is a human phenomenon.

Scapegoating is not a historical anomaly.

It is one of civilization's oldest technologies.

The specific context of this story is Black America, but the underlying mechanism is universal.

Every society has a version of this story.

Every family has a version of this story.

Every institution has a version of this story.

Many individuals do as well.

Perhaps that is why I ultimately chose the phase-change metaphor.

Water does not cease to exist when it becomes vapor.

It changes state.

The container loses its ability to hold it.

In many ways, this novella asks whether societies can experience something similar.

What happens when people stop occupying the role assigned to them?

What happens when a system loses its preferred explanation?

What happens when visibility itself becomes a form of participation that can be withdrawn?

I have no definitive answers.

The older I get, the less interested I become in certainty.

What interests me now are questions capable of surviving disagreement.

Questions that remain alive long after the book has been closed.

Questions that continue generating dialogue between readers who see the world differently.

If this novella succeeds, it will not succeed because readers agree with it.

It will succeed because readers argue with it.

Because they challenge it.

Because they discuss it with people they care about.

Because they find themselves returning to its central question months or years later.

Stories rarely change the world.

At best, they alter the questions we ask while moving through it.

This novella is my attempt to contribute one such question:

What remains when the explanation disappears?

The answer, if one exists, belongs not to the author, but to the reader.

— *Alphonse Damas*
