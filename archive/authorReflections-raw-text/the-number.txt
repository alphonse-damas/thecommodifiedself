Author's Reflections
The Number

When I first imagined the world of The Number, I was not thinking about sex.

I was thinking about metrics.

We live in an age increasingly obsessed with measurement.

Followers.

Likes.

Credit scores.

Performance reviews.

Standardized tests.

Net worth.

Body counts.

Engagement statistics.

Algorithms quietly transform complex human beings into digestible quantities. We are measured at work, online, in school, and increasingly in our personal lives. More and more, our value is expressed not through story, character, or context, but through numbers.

The obvious question became:

What happens when the most private aspect of human life becomes the most public?

The Number emerged from that question.

At first glance, the premise appears to be about sexuality. A visible count of every sexual encounter a person has experienced. Yet beneath the surface, the story is investigating something much larger.

What fascinates me is not the Number itself.

It is what people do with it.

A Number, by itself, is neutral.

It contains no morality.

No context.

No motive.

No history.

Yet human beings are incapable of leaving a Number alone. We immediately begin constructing narratives around it. We interpret. We judge. We classify.

A man with a high Number becomes one thing.

A woman with the identical Number becomes another.

A person with a low Number is admired by some, pitied by others.

The Number never changes.

Only the observer does.

That distinction sits at the heart of this novel.

As I developed the world, I became increasingly interested in how different groups would experience the same metric differently. Gender quickly emerged as an obvious fault line. Throughout history, identical sexual behaviors have often been interpreted differently depending on who performs them.

But race complicated the question further.

Would a Black woman carrying a high Number be judged the same way as a White woman?

Would a Black man with a high Number be celebrated, feared, or both simultaneously?

Would stereotypes simply migrate into the new system?

I suspect they would.

Human beings have an extraordinary ability to take supposedly neutral systems and use them to reinforce existing assumptions.

History demonstrates this repeatedly.

The Number simply gives society another instrument.

The same song plays.

Only the technology changes.

This realization led me to one of the central themes of the novel:

Transparency does not eliminate prejudice.

Many people assume that if we simply had more information, society would become fairer.

The Number tests that assumption.

What if everyone possessed perfect information?

Would we become more compassionate?

Or would we simply become more efficient at judging one another?

I suspect the latter.

That suspicion gave birth to The Veil.

Every system eventually produces a resistance movement. The Veil emerged not because the Number was false, but because it was true in a dangerously incomplete way.

A Number counts.

A story explains.

The conflict between those two ideas drives much of the novel.

The Veil believes the Number has become a prison.

The Council believes it has become liberation.

Elijah and Tala find themselves trapped somewhere between those positions.

Their relationship became the emotional center of the story because each represents a different form of social judgment.

Elijah's zero is treated as absence.

Tala's three hundred sixty-seven is treated as excess.

One is defined by what society believes he lacks.

The other by what society believes she has too much of.

Yet both suffer from the same problem.

Neither is seen.

The world sees their Numbers.

The world sees its assumptions.

The world rarely sees them.

That felt profoundly human to me.

In many ways, all of us carry invisible Numbers.

Not literal ones.

But measurements assigned by others.

Success.

Failure.

Beauty.

Intelligence.

Status.

Respectability.

We spend enormous portions of our lives negotiating those labels.

Some embrace them.

Some resist them.

Some spend years trying to escape them.

The Number merely makes visible a process that already exists.

Perhaps that is why speculative fiction interests me so deeply.

The best speculative fiction does not predict the future.

It exaggerates the present.

It takes a current trend and asks what happens if we continue walking in that direction.

The Number asks what happens when measurement becomes identity.

When data becomes destiny.

When transparency becomes tyranny.

Most importantly, it asks whether human beings are capable of seeing one another beyond the metrics that define us.

I do not pretend to know the answer.

In fact, I suspect the answer changes with every generation.

What I do know is this:

A person's life can never be reduced to a score.

A number can tell us what happened.

It cannot tell us what it meant.

And meaning is where humanity lives.

That is the space this novel attempts to defend.

Not privacy.

Not sexuality.

Not even freedom.

But the irreducible complexity of a human being.

Because once we begin confusing measurement with understanding, we risk forgetting something essential:

People are stories.

And stories have always been larger than numbers.

— Alphonse Damas
